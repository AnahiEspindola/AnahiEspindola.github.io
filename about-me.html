About me

I am from Argentina (Santa Fe, to be more precise) but I currently live and work in beautiful Moscow, ID (USA). I studied Biology at the National University of Córdoba (Argentina) and at the University of Neuchatel (Switzerland). I obtained my Masters in Evolutionary Entomology at the E-vol lab at the University of Neuchatel, working on the populations genetics of tri-tropic interactions (plant-herbivore-parasitoid interactions).

My Doctoral research investigated the comparative phylogeography of obligate and specialized plant-pollinator interactions in Europe. In particular, I could perform natural history, field and molecular research on two super exciting pollination systems: Arum maculatum and its lurring traps, and the nursery pollination system formed by Trollius europaeus and Chiastocheta flies.

I continued working with these and other specialized pollination systems during a short post-doc in the Alvarez lab, at the University of Lausanne.

Thanks to two post-doctoral grants from the Swiss National Science Foundation, I could start my own independent research line. In collaboration with Scott Nuismer and Jack Sullivan, I now investigate the effect of the environment on the spatio-temporal dynamics of plant-insect interactions. To do this, I use the specialized oil-rewarding pollination interaction displayed between several Calceolaria species and their oil-collecting insects as models. My research tools consist of combinations of field surveys, experimental approaches in greenhouse conditions, phylogeographic analyses and morphometric methods.

Since 2015, I am also working with Jack Sullivan, Dave Tank and Bryan Carstens to study the comparative phylogeography of the temperate rainforests of the US Pacific Northwest, as well as developing tools to identify potential cryptic diversity in disjunct biomes.

Take a look at my CV to learn more about me!
